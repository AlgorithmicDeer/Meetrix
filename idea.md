# Meeting Waste Observatory

## The Problem

Organisations lose an enormous amount of productive time to meetings. Most of this loss is invisible — it never appears on a balance sheet, nobody tracks it, and the people causing it have no idea they are doing so. The cost is distributed across hundreds of individuals and thousands of hours, which makes it easy to ignore until it is too late.

The problem is not that meetings exist. Meetings serve a purpose. The problem is that organisations have no instrument for measuring which meetings are working and which are not. Without measurement, nothing changes. The same recurring meetings continue indefinitely. The same people sit in rooms that do not need them. The same decisions get deferred because the wrong group assembled.

## What Makes This Hard

Meeting quality is subjective and uncomfortable to challenge directly. A recurring meeting exists because someone set it up — often someone with authority — and cancelling it requires either confrontation or political capital most people are unwilling to spend. So instead of being cancelled, bad meetings accumulate. They grow. They spawn sub-meetings. They consume the calendar capacity of the most valuable people in the organisation.

The data to diagnose this exists. It lives in calendars, in meeting acceptance patterns, in the ratio of participants to decisions made, in the gap between who is invited and who actually contributes. Nobody synthesises this data because doing so manually would take longer than the meetings themselves.

## The Opportunity

If an organisation could see — clearly and quantifiably — exactly how much time and money flows into meetings that produce nothing, the conversation changes. It moves from opinion to evidence. Instead of "I think we have too many meetings," a leader can say "we spent £340,000 last quarter on meetings that generated zero documented decisions." That number demands a response.

Beyond the headline figure, the real value is specificity. Which meeting types are the problem? Which teams are in meeting overload? Which individuals are so calendared they cannot do the work the meetings are supposed to enable? Which recurring meetings have outlived their purpose and exist only by inertia?

A system that answers these questions — clearly, continuously, and without requiring anyone to manually audit their own calendar — would give organisations the instrument they have never had.

## What Success Looks Like

An organisation uses this system and within a week has a clear picture of where meeting time is being wasted and why. Leaders can see the cost in real numbers. Teams can see their own patterns. The system surfaces specific recommendations: meetings to cancel, merge, shorten, or restructure. Over time, it tracks whether interventions actually improved things or just shifted the problem.

The output is not a report that gets read once and filed. It is a living observatory that makes the invisible visible — and keeps it visible until the behaviour changes.
